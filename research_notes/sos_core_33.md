### 3.3 Canonization: the syntactic invariant, computed

Definition 3.4 leaves two debts. It reads a lasso through one presentation, and a
lasso has many — nothing yet says all presentations receive one verdict. And it
evaluates whatever invariant it is handed — nothing yet singles out, among the many
invariants a language admits, a canonical one. Both debts are paid on the invariants
themselves, in three steps: the *law* an acceptance layer must obey for the verdict
to be presentation-independent; the *target*, one invariant per language, built on
Arnold's congruence [Arn85]; and the *move* that reaches it — `reduce`, a
canonization of any invariant, computed by right multiplications alone, in
polynomial time.

Throughout this section the algebra is **generated**: every class is the fold of
some word. Nothing is lost: the folds form a submonoid, no other class is ever
consulted by a query, and one breadth-first walk of the letter actions restricts an
arbitrary algebra to its generated part. We write `𝒲 := ⟦Σ⁺⟧` for the **word
classes**, the folds of nonempty words; `𝒲` is closed under `M`, and in the
canonical invariants built below `𝒲 = 𝒞 ∖ {[ε]}`, though an arbitrary algebra may
fold nonempty words onto its identity.

**One lasso, many names.** Every presentation folds to a pair: say `(u, v)`
**folds to** `(⟦u⟧·e, e)`, `e := ⟦v⟧^ω` — the pair Definition 3.4 queries — and
say a pair **names** the lasso `u·v^ω` when one of its presentations folds to it.
A name is a linked pair (`e² = e` by construction, and `(⟦u⟧·e)·e = ⟦u⟧·e`), and
both coordinates are folds of nonempty words: every name lies in `𝒲 × 𝒲`. A loop
may be **rotated**: a factor carried from the loop's front onto the stem leaves
the ω-word unchanged, `u·g·(h·g)^ω = u·(g·h)^ω` — and rotation is the one move
that changes a lasso's name.

**Lemma 3.5 (rotation).** Let `𝒜` be a generated algebra and `s, g, h ∈ 𝒞` with
`g·h ∈ 𝒲` and `s·(gh)^ω = s`. Then `(s·g, (hg)^ω)` is a linked pair, and some
lasso is named by both `(s, (gh)^ω)` and `(s·g, (hg)^ω)`.

*Proof.* First the algebra identities. Associativity gives `g·(hg)^m = (gh)^m·g`
for every `m ≥ 1`. Pick `k₁, k₂ ≥ 1` with `(gh)^{k₁} = (gh)^ω` and
`(hg)^{k₂} = (hg)^ω`, and set `m := k₁·k₂`: then `(gh)^m = ((gh)^{k₁})^{k₂} =
(gh)^ω` and likewise `(hg)^m = (hg)^ω`, so `g·(hg)^ω = (gh)^ω·g`. Hence `(hg)^ω`
is idempotent and `(s·g)·(hg)^ω = s·(gh)^ω·g = s·g`: the rotated pair is linked.
Now pick words `p, q` with `⟦p⟧ = g`, `⟦q⟧ = h` and `p·q` nonempty — possible
because `𝒜` is generated: a class other than `[ε]` has only nonempty preimages,
and if `g = h = [ε]` then `g·h = [ε] ∈ 𝒲` supplies a nonempty preimage to use as
`p`. Pick also `w` with `⟦w⟧ = s`, and consider the single ω-word
`α := w·(pq)^ω`. The presentation `(w, (pq)^m)` folds to
`(s·(gh)^ω, (gh)^ω) = (s, (gh)^ω)`; the presentation `(w·p, (qp)^m)` — the same
ω-word, `w·(pq)^ω = w·p·(qp)^ω` — folds to
`(s·g·(hg)^ω, (hg)^ω) = (s·g, (hg)^ω)`. So `α` is named by both. ∎

Call two linked pairs **conjugate** when rotations connect them — the equivalence
generated by `(s, (gh)^ω) ∼ (s·g, (hg)^ω)`; the notion is classical
[PP04, Ch. II, Prop. 2.6]. Stem extension is the degenerate rotation
`g = h = ⟦v⟧`: the loop's value is unchanged and the stem absorbs one turn — why
`(u, v)` and `(uv, v)` may name one lasso by two pairs.

**Definition 3.6 (saturation).** A pair set `P` over a generated algebra is
**saturated** when it is closed under conjugacy: for all `s, g, h ∈ 𝒞` with
`g·h ∈ 𝒲` and `s·(gh)^ω = s`,

```
    (s, (gh)^ω) ∈ P   ⟺   (s·g, (hg)^ω) ∈ P.
```

Saturation is table-checkable — finitely many triples `(s, g, h)`, each one
product and two lookups — and it is the exact law Definition 3.4 was owed. The
missing half is that *all* names of one lasso are conjugate, and it is a chase of
three moves:

**Lemma 3.7 (the names of a lasso).** In a generated algebra, any two names of
one lasso are conjugate.

*Proof.* Three preliminary observations. *(Powering.)* For `k ≥ 1`, the
presentations `(u, v)` and `(u, v^k)` fold to one pair: the powers of
`⟦v^k⟧ = ⟦v⟧^k` are powers of `⟦v⟧`, each set holds exactly one idempotent, so
`⟦v^k⟧^ω = ⟦v⟧^ω =: e`; and the stems agree, `⟦u⟧·e` both. *(Absorption.)*
`e·⟦v⟧ = ⟦v⟧·e` — powers of one element commute. *(One turn.)* For any `s` with
`s·e = s`, the rotation at `(s, ⟦v⟧, ⟦v⟧)` — legal, since `⟦v⟧² ∈ 𝒲` and
`(⟦v⟧²)^ω = e` — connects `(s, e)` to `(s·⟦v⟧, e)`.

Now let `(u, v)` and `(u', v')` present one lasso `w`, say `|u| ≤ |u'|`
(conjugacy is symmetric). Both stems are prefixes of `w` and the tail after `u`
is `v^ω`, so `u' = u·z` with `z` a prefix of `v^ω`: write `z = v^q·v₁` with
`0 ≤ |v₁| < |v|`, and split `v = v₁·v₂` (`v₂` nonempty). The tail of `w` after
`u'` is then `(v₂·v₁)^ω`, so `(u', v₂v₁)` is a third presentation of `w`.

*The names of `(u, v)` and `(u', v₂v₁)` are conjugate.* Choose `m ≥ 1` with
`⟦v⟧^m = e` and `(⟦v₂⟧⟦v₁⟧)^m = (⟦v₂⟧⟦v₁⟧)^ω =: f` simultaneously (the product
trick of Lemma 3.5). Applying *(one turn)* `q` times connects `(⟦u⟧·e, e)`, the
name `(u, v)` folds to, with `(⟦u⟧·⟦v⟧^q·e, e)`, the name `(u·v^q, v)` folds to.
If `v₁ = ε` that presentation is `(u', v)` and `v₂v₁ = v`: done. Otherwise one
rotation at the triple `(⟦u⟧⟦v⟧^q·e, ⟦v₁⟧, ⟦v₂⟧)` — legal, since
`⟦v₁⟧·⟦v₂⟧ = ⟦v⟧ ∈ 𝒲` and the stem absorbs `⟦v⟧^ω = e` — connects it further to
`(⟦u⟧⟦v⟧^q·e·⟦v₁⟧, f)`, and that pair is the name `(u', v₂v₁)` folds to: by
*(absorption)* and `g·(hg)^m = (gh)^m·g`,

```
    ⟦u⟧⟦v⟧^q·e·⟦v₁⟧  =  ⟦u⟧⟦v⟧^{q+m}·⟦v₁⟧  =  ⟦u⟧⟦v⟧^q·⟦v₁⟧·(⟦v₂⟧⟦v₁⟧)^m  =  ⟦u'⟧·f.
```

*The names of `(u', v₂v₁)` and `(u', v')` are equal.* Both loops repeat to the
same tail, `(v₂v₁)^ω = v'^ω`; the prefix of that tail of length `|v'|·|v₂v₁|` is
a common power, `v'^{|v₂v₁|} = (v₂v₁)^{|v'|}` as finite words, and *(powering)*
twice gives one name for all four presentations `(u', v')`, `(u', v'^{|v₂v₁|})`,
`(u', (v₂v₁)^{|v'|})` and `(u', v₂v₁)`. Chaining: the names of `(u, v)` and
`(u', v')` are conjugate. ∎

**Theorem 3.8 (admissibility).** Let `𝓘 = ⟨𝒜, P⟩` be an invariant, `𝒜`
generated. The query of Definition 3.4 gives every presentation of every lasso
one verdict iff `P` is saturated. Call such an `𝓘` **admissible**; its accepted
lassos are then exactly the lassos of the regular language

```
    L(𝓘)  :=  ⋃ { fold⁻¹(s)·(fold⁻¹(e) ∩ Σ⁺)^ω  :  (s, e) ∈ P,  e·e = e,  s·e = s },
```

the unique regular language with those lassos.

*Proof.* *(Saturated ⟹ one verdict.)* Two presentations of one lasso fold to
conjugate names (Lemma 3.7); a saturated `P` is closed under the steps that
generate conjugacy, so conjugate pairs are both in or both out of `P` — one
verdict.

*(One verdict ⟹ saturated.)* Let `(s, g, h)` satisfy `g·h ∈ 𝒲` and
`s·(gh)^ω = s`. Lemma 3.5 exhibits one lasso and two of its presentations
folding to `(s, (gh)^ω)` and `(s·g, (hg)^ω)`; one verdict on that lasso puts the
two pairs on one side of `P`.

*(The accepted lassos are the lassos of `L(𝓘)`.)* Let `w` be accepted: every
presentation `(u, v)` folds to one pair `(s, e) ∈ P`, `e := ⟦v⟧^ω`. Pick `k`
with `⟦v⟧^k = e`: the stem block `u·v^k` folds to `⟦u⟧·⟦v⟧^k = ⟦u⟧·e = s`, the
loop blocks `v^k` fold to `e`, so `w = (u·v^k)·(v^k)^ω` lies in the member of
the union indexed by `(s, e)`. Conversely let `w` be a lasso of `L(𝓘)`:
`w = w₀·w₁·w₂⋯` with `⟦w₀⟧ = s`, `⟦w_j⟧ = e` for `j ≥ 1`, `(s, e) ∈ P`,
`e² = e`, `s·e = s`; and let `(u, v)` present `w`. The block boundaries
`b_j := |w₀⋯w_j|` are infinitely many, so two of them, `m := b_j < m' := b_{j'}`,
lie beyond `|u|` and agree modulo `|v|`. The prefix `w[0, m) = w₀⋯w_j` folds to
`s·e^j = s`. Position `m` sits in the `v`-periodic tail at phase
`r := (m − |u|) mod |v|`: split `v = v₁·v₂` with `|v₁| = r`; the tail of `w`
after `m` is `(v₂v₁)^ω`, and `m' − m` is a multiple of `|v|`, so the segment
`w[m, m') = (v₂v₁)^{(m'−m)/|v|}` — nonempty, and folding to `e^{j'−j} = e`.
Thus `(w[0, m), w[m, m'))` is a presentation of `w` whose loop folds to the
idempotent `e` directly: it folds to `(s·e, e) = (s, e) ∈ P`. One of `w`'s
presentations accepts, hence — one verdict — all do.

*(Regularity and uniqueness.)* Each `fold⁻¹(c)` is recognized by the finite
monoid `(𝒞, M)` through the fold morphism (§2), hence regular; `L(𝓘)` is a
finite union of sets `X·Y^ω` with `X, Y` regular — ω-rational, hence regular
[PP04, Ch. II, Thm 7.5]. Two regular ω-languages with the same lassos are equal
[PP04, Ch. I, Cor. 9.8]. ∎

An invariant is a *representation*, then, exactly when it is generated and
saturated. What is still missing is one representation per language, and the
classifying relation is Arnold's [Arn85]. A finite word sits in a lasso either
in the stem or inside the loop, and interchangeability must hold in both
positions:

**Definition 3.9 (syntactic congruence [Arn85]).** Two words `u, v ∈ Σ⁺` are
**syntactically congruent** for `L`, written `u ≈_L v`, when they are
interchangeable in both context shapes:

```
    (linear)     ∀ x, y ∈ Σ*, t ∈ Σ⁺ :   x·u·y·t^ω ∈ L  ⟺  x·v·y·t^ω ∈ L
    (ω-power)    ∀ x, y ∈ Σ*          :   x·(u·y)^ω ∈ L  ⟺  x·(v·y)^ω ∈ L
```

The linear shape mutates the stem — a finite change, a loop `t` appended to complete
the lasso; the ω-power shape mutates inside the loop, where the change recurs
forever. `≈_L` is a two-sided **congruence** — `u ≈_L v` gives `x·u·y ≈_L x·v·y` —
of **finite index** for regular `L`: finitely many classes [Arn85]. And it is the
**coarsest** relation with these interchange properties — every relation
interchangeable in both shapes refines it — the first of two senses in which the
quotient below is minimal.

*Example.* On Figure 1 (`aUGb`), from `L = a*·b^ω` alone: `a ≉_L b` by the ω-power
shape at `x = y = ε` — `a^ω ∉ L`, `b^ω ∈ L`; `ab ≉_L ba` by the linear shape at
`x = y = ε`, `t = b` — `ab·b^ω ∈ L`, `ba·b^ω ∉ L`; and `a ≈_L aa` — membership in
`L` never counts `a`'s, in either shape a block of `a`'s acts as one `a`. The
quotient `Σ⁺/≈_L` has exactly four classes — `a⁺`, `b⁺`, `a⁺b⁺` and the dead words —
the four boxes of Figure 1 other than `[ε]`.

**Definition 3.10 (the invariant of `L`).** `𝓘(L) := ⟨𝒜(L), P(L)⟩`, where
`𝒜(L) = (𝒞, λ, M)` and:

- `𝒞 := Σ⁺/≈_L ∪ {[ε]}`: one **word class** per congruence class of nonempty
  words, keyed by its shortlex-smallest member (§3.1), plus the adjoined `[ε]`;
- `λ(x) := [x]` for `x ∈ Σ`, and `λ(ε) := [ε]`;
- `M` is the induced product, `[u]·[v] := [u·v]` on word classes — well defined
  because `≈_L` is a two-sided congruence — with `[ε]` the identity;
- `P(L) := { (s, e) : s, e word classes, e·e = e, s·e = s, w_s·(w_e)^ω ∈ L }`,
  where `w_s` and `w_e` are the keys of `s` and `e` — the linked pairs (§2, now of
  classes) whose one representative lasso belongs to `L`.

`𝒜(L)` is an algebra in the sense of Definition 3.1 — `[ε]` is isolated, no letter
folding to it — and it is generated, with `𝒲 = 𝒞 ∖ {[ε]}`: `⟦u⟧ = [u]` for every
`u ∈ Σ*` (`λ` seeds the letters, `M` is the induced product, induction does the
rest), so every class is the fold of each of its members, and `⟦u⟧ = [ε]` only for
`u = ε`. As stated, `P(L)` consults a single lasso per pair, built from the keys;
that the choice of representatives is innocent is Theorem 3.12's content.

*Example.* Figure 1 is `𝓘(aUGb)` — §2 called the drawing a syntactic ω-semigroup,
and Definition 3.10 is that claim made precise. Six pairs of word classes are
linked: `([a],[a])`, `([b],[b])`, `([a·b],[b])`, `([b·a],[a])`, `([b·a],[b])`,
`([b·a],[b·a])`. Testing each key lasso: `b·b^ω = b^ω ∈ L` and `ab·b^ω ∈ L`; the
four others fail — `a·a^ω = a^ω` never shows a `b`, and a dead stem stays dead. So
`P(L) = { ([b],[b]), ([a·b],[b]) }`, the pair set printed beneath the figure.

The two shapes were tailored to lassos, and they pay immediately:

**Lemma 3.11 (substitution).** If `u ≈_L u'` and `v ≈_L v'` (all four words
nonempty), then `u·v^ω ∈ L ⟺ u'·v'^ω ∈ L`.

*Proof.* Swap the loop: the ω-power shape of `v ≈_L v'`, at `x = u` and `y = ε`,
gives `u·v^ω ∈ L ⟺ u·v'^ω ∈ L`. Swap the stem: the linear shape of `u ≈_L u'`, at
`x = y = ε` and `t = v'`, gives `u·v'^ω ∈ L ⟺ u'·v'^ω ∈ L`. ∎

**Theorem 3.12 (canonicity).** For every regular ω-language `L`:

(i) `𝓘(L)` is admissible and `L(𝓘(L)) = L`: on `𝓘(L)`, the query of
Definition 3.4 answers membership in `L` itself, whatever the presentation;

(ii) `𝓘` is a **complete invariant**: two regular ω-languages over `Σ` are equal
iff `𝓘(L)` and `𝓘(L')` are identical, component by component — byte equality once
serialized, keys and all.

*Proof.* (i) Let `(u, v)` present the lasso `w`, `v` nonempty; the query computes
`e := ⟦v⟧^ω` and `s := ⟦u⟧·e`. The idempotent power is a power: pick `k ≥ 1` with
`⟦v⟧^k = e`. Rewrite `w` on the presentation `(u·v^k, v^k)`: the fold is a morphism
(§3.2), so `⟦u·v^k⟧ = ⟦u⟧·e = s` and `⟦v^k⟧ = e` — the queried pair is unchanged,
and both coordinates are now folds of nonempty words. Since `𝒜(L)` is generated
with `⟦u⟧ = [u]` (Definition 3.10), `s = [u·v^k]` and `e = [v^k]`: the keys satisfy
`w_s ≈_L u·v^k` and `w_e ≈_L v^k`. Lemma 3.11 substitutes both at once:
`w = (u·v^k)·(v^k)^ω ∈ L ⟺ w_s·(w_e)^ω ∈ L`, and the right-hand side is, by
definition, `(s, e) ∈ P(L)`. The query's verdict is membership in `L`, whatever
the presentation — in particular one verdict per lasso, so `P(L)` is saturated
(Theorem 3.8) and `𝓘(L)` is admissible; and `L(𝓘(L))` and `L` are then regular
languages with the same lassos, hence equal [PP04, Ch. I, Cor. 9.8].

(ii) `≈_L`, the shortlex keys, `λ`, `M` and the membership tests of `P(L)` are
functions of `L` alone, so `L = L'` gives `𝓘(L) = 𝓘(L')` literally. Conversely,
by (i), `𝓘(L) = 𝓘(L')` gives `L = L(𝓘(L)) = L(𝓘(L')) = L'`. ∎

*Example.* On Figure 1 (`aUGb`), present `aab·b^ω` as `(aab, b)` or as
`(aabb, bb)`: both fold to the pair `([a·b], [b])` — here even the *name* is
stable. That is a feature of `aUGb`, not of the theorem: `Even` (§3.4) answers
one lasso through two distinct names — conjugate, as Lemma 3.7 promises — and
saturation of `P(Even)` is what forces them to one verdict.

Canonicity hands every language one invariant; canonization is the road back from
all the others. Given an admissible `𝓘`, the components of `𝓘(L(𝓘))` are
computable from `𝓘`'s table — and the apparent obstruction is that Definition 3.9
is two-sided: a word sits between a left context and a right one, while a table
offers one operation for free, multiply on the right. Rotation closes the gap with
data instead of left multiplications: a left context contributes no information of
its own, it only shifts the *slot* where a right test is read — so each class is
seeded with its verdicts at every slot, and refined on the right.

**Definition 3.13 (profile; reduce).** Let `𝓘` be admissible, `n := |𝒞|`, and
write `Val(c, d) := ((c·d^ω, d^ω) ∈ P)` for `c ∈ 𝒞, d ∈ 𝒲` — the totalized query:
by Theorem 3.8, the verdict of every lasso whose stem folds to `c` and whose loop
folds to `d`. The **profile** of a word class `c ∈ 𝒲` is its verdict data at every
slot:

```
    prof(c)  :=  ( (Val(x·c, t))_{x ∈ 𝒞, t ∈ 𝒲} ,   (Val(x, c))_{x ∈ 𝒞} )
```

— as a stem, the verdict row of every left translate of `c`; as a loop, the
verdict column of `c` against every stem. `reduce(𝓘)` is computed by partition
refinement on `𝒲`, the identity held out:

1. *Seed* by equal profiles.
2. *Refine on the right*: split blocks until `c ∼ c'` implies
   `c·λ(a) ∼ c'·λ(a)` for every letter `a` — Moore rounds, recomputing each
   class's letter-successor block vector and re-partitioning, at most `n` rounds
   of `O(n·|Σ|)`, since a round short of the fixpoint splits a block. Right
   letters suffice: right contexts are letter products, and the profiles carry
   the left contexts as slots — no context triple is ever enumerated.
3. *Quotient*: blocks become word classes; `λ` and `M` are induced; a fresh
   identity `[ε]` is adjoined — never merged, even into a block that acts
   neutrally; the pair set is the image of `P`'s linked pairs; and each class is
   re-keyed by the shortlex-least word folding into it, one breadth-first walk of
   the letter actions.

**Theorem 3.14 (canonization).** For every admissible `𝓘`:
`reduce(𝓘) = 𝓘(L(𝓘))`, byte for byte. The computation is polynomial in the input:
`O(n²)` `Val` cells behind the profiles — each one product, one memoized
idempotent walk, one lookup — then at most `n` refinement rounds of `O(n·|Σ|)`.

*Proof.* Write `L := L(𝓘)`, `≈` for `≈_L` (Definition 3.9), `Π` for the fixpoint
partition, and `π` for the quotient map. By Theorem 3.8, for all `x, y ∈ Σ*`,
`t ∈ Σ⁺`, `w ∈ Σ⁺`:

```
    x·w·y·t^ω ∈ L ⟺ Val(⟦x⟧·⟦w⟧·⟦y⟧, ⟦t⟧)        x·(w·y)^ω ∈ L ⟺ Val(⟦x⟧, ⟦w⟧·⟦y⟧)
```

— the verdict of a lasso is a function of its folds. Three consequences anchor
the proof.

*(a) Same fold, congruent words.* `⟦w⟧ = ⟦w'⟧` implies `w ≈ w'`: both displays
depend on `w` through `⟦w⟧` alone. The relation `c ≡ c'` :⟺ "the members of `c`
are `≈`-congruent to the members of `c'`" is therefore a well-defined equivalence
on `𝒲`, and, the algebra being generated, `[w]_≈ ↦ (block of ⟦w⟧)` is a bijection
between `Σ⁺/≈` and `𝒲/≡`: well defined and injective by the definition of `≡`,
surjective because every class is a fold.

*(b) `≡` refines the seed and is right-stable.* Let `c ≡ c'` and `w ∈ c`,
`w' ∈ c'`, so `w ≈ w'`. Every slot is a fold, `x = ⟦y⟧` with `y ∈ Σ*`, and every
loop argument is a fold of a nonempty word, `t = ⟦z⟧`: `Val(x·c, t)` and
`Val(x·c', t)` are the verdicts of `y·w·z^ω` and `y·w'·z^ω`, equal by the linear
shape of `w ≈ w'` at `(y, ε, z)`; `Val(x, c)` and `Val(x, c')` are the verdicts
of `y·w^ω` and `y·w'^ω`, equal by the ω-power shape at `(y, ε)`. So
`prof(c) = prof(c')`. Right stability: `w·a ≈ w'·a` since `≈` is a congruence, so
`c·λ(a) ≡ c'·λ(a)`.

*(c) The fixpoint is `≡`.* By (b) and induction over the rounds, `≡` refines
every intermediate partition — a split never separates an `≡`-related pair —
hence `≡` refines `Π`. Conversely let `c Π c'`, `w ∈ c`, `w' ∈ c'`; both shapes
of Definition 3.9 must be checked. For any `y ∈ Σ*`, right stability of the
fixpoint gives `c·⟦y⟧ Π c'·⟦y⟧` (induction on `|y|`), and `Π` refines the seed,
so `prof(c·⟦y⟧) = prof(c'·⟦y⟧)`. Linear shape, at `x ∈ Σ*`, `t ∈ Σ⁺`: the
verdicts of `x·w·y·t^ω` and `x·w'·y·t^ω` are `Val(⟦x⟧·(c·⟦y⟧), ⟦t⟧)` and
`Val(⟦x⟧·(c'·⟦y⟧), ⟦t⟧)` — the stem halves of the two profiles at slot `⟦x⟧`,
equal. ω-power shape, at `x ∈ Σ*`: the verdicts of `x·(wy)^ω` and `x·(w'y)^ω`
are `Val(⟦x⟧, c·⟦y⟧)` and `Val(⟦x⟧, c'·⟦y⟧)` — the loop halves, equal. So
`w ≈ w'`, that is, `c ≡ c'`.

*(Components.)* By (a) and (c), the blocks of `Π` are in bijection with
`Σ⁺/≈_L`, each block holding the folds of exactly one congruence class — whose
members are exactly the words folding into the block. The induced product and
letter map coincide with Definition 3.10's, the fresh identity matches the
adjoined `[ε]`, and the key of a block — the shortlex-least word folding into
it — is the shortlex-least member of its congruence class: Definition 3.10's
key. For the pair set, `Val` is block data: `c Π c₂` gives `Val(c, t) = Val(c₂, t)`
(stem halves at slot `[ε]`) and `d Π d₂` gives `Val(x, d) = Val(x, d₂)` (loop
halves), so `Val(c, d) = Val(c₂, d₂)` whenever `c Π c₂` and `d Π d₂`. A linked
pair `(S, E)` of the quotient lies in `P(L(𝓘))` iff its key lasso lies in `L`,
iff `Val(c, d)` holds at the folds `c, d` of its keys — members of the blocks
`S, E` — iff `Val(s, e)` holds for any `s ∈ S`, `e ∈ E`. For `(s, e) ∈ P`
linked, `Val(s, e) = ((s·e, e) ∈ P)` is true, and its image is
`(π(s)·π(e), π(e)) = (S·E, E) = (S, E)` — so every image pair is in `P(L(𝓘))`;
conversely `(S, E) ∈ P(L(𝓘))` gives `Val(c, d)` true at its key folds, that is,
`(c·d^ω, d^ω) ∈ P` — a linked member of `P` whose image is `(S, E)`, since `π`
commutes with products and hence with idempotent powers. The image pair set is
`P(L(𝓘))`. Identical components, identical keys: byte equality with
Definition 3.10.

*(Cost.)* The `n²` cells of `Val` need the idempotent powers — one `O(n)` power
walk per class, memoized — then one product and one membership lookup each. The
profiles compare by hashing verdict rows once; each Moore round recomputes `n`
letter-successor block vectors of size `|Σ|`; and at most `n` rounds run, since
each round short of the fixpoint splits a block. ∎

**Corollary 3.15.** (i) `reduce` is idempotent, and the syntactic invariants are
exactly its fixpoints among admissible invariants: `reduce(𝓘(L)) = 𝓘(L)`.
(ii) Equivalence is decidable within the representation, in polynomial time:
`L(𝓘₁) = L(𝓘₂)` iff `reduce(𝓘₁)` and `reduce(𝓘₂)` are byte-equal.
(iii) Every admissible invariant of `L` maps onto `𝓘(L)` by the quotient of
Theorem 3.14 — `𝓘(L)` is minimal among the invariants of `L`, class count
included: the second sense of minimality, completing Definition 3.9's first.

*Proof.* (i) `𝓘(L)` is admissible with `L(𝓘(L)) = L` (Theorem 3.12), so
Theorem 3.14 gives `reduce(𝓘(L)) = 𝓘(L)`; and every `reduce` output is a
syntactic invariant. (ii) Theorem 3.12(ii) applied to the reducts, Theorem 3.14
pricing them. (iii) The quotient map of Theorem 3.14 is onto and carries `P`
onto `P(L)`. ∎

*Example.* On Figure 1's table, replace `P` by all six linked pairs of word
classes: the invariant is admissible — conjugacy never leaves the linked
pairs — and accepts every lasso. Every profile is all-true, the seed is a single
block, no letter splits it, and the quotient is the two-class invariant of
`Σ^ω`: one word class, keyed `a`, with `P = {([a], [a])}`. Five classes were
presentation; canonization says so.

Rotation has now served twice, and the division of labor is the summary of the
section. As a law on `P`, it delimits the invariants that mean anything at all
(Theorem 3.8); as a computation scheme, it is why the profiles need only right
refinement — a left context contributes no information of its own, it only
shifts the slot where a right test is read. (On a table, where a left translate
is itself one lookup, the same fixpoint is reached from the empty-context
signature `((Val(c, t))_t, (Val(x, c))_x)` by splitting under letters on both
sides — the implementation's form; the slot-indexed profile is the shape that
survives when left products are not on offer, which is §4's situation.) The
rotation identity itself is classical — at the algebra it is Wilke's axiom
`s·(ts)^ω = (st)^ω` [PP04, Ch. II, Thm 5.1], and conjugacy of linked pairs
organizes the classical theory: the general statement behind Lemma 3.7 is that
any two Ramseyan factorizations of one ω-word land on conjugate pairs
[PP04, Ch. II, Prop. 2.8, Cor. 2.9]. What this paper draws from the identity is
a different service: canonization by right multiplications, polynomial on the
representation. §4 instantiates the engine on an automaton — there the slot
space collapses from the algebra to the machine's states, and the construction
of `𝓘(L)` from any deterministic acceptor is: build *some* admissible invariant,
and reduce it.
